# Knowledge: Military Intelligence & Strategy

Domain-specific knowledge base for dispatches and discussions about war, strategy, espionage, Musashi, Burnham, and geopolitics.

## Key Frameworks

### Sun Tzu — The Art of War
- **Winning without fighting** as the highest form of strategy
- **Knowing the enemy and knowing yourself** — information asymmetry as competitive advantage
- **Deception** as structural, not moral: "All warfare is based on deception"
- **Terrain and positioning** — context determines strategy, not the reverse

### Miyamoto Musashi — Book of Five Rings
- **The Void (相)** — not emptiness, but the space where patterns haven't solidified
- **No-stance stance** — freedom from fixed positions, adaptability as ultimate strategy
- **"Do nothing that is of no use"** — operational simplicity
- **Timing** — knowing when to act and when to wait are equally important
- **The way of the void** — seeing through appearances to underlying structure

### John Boyd — Patterns of Conflict
- **OODA loop** — Observe-Orient-Decide-Act as universal decision framework
- **Orientation is the key step** — 80% of his emphasis was on how we interpret observations
- **Getting inside the opponent's loop** — make them react to stale situations
- **Moral warfare** — attacking the will to fight, not just the capability
- **Fingerspitzengefühl** — intuitive feel developed through deep experience

### Samuel Burnham — Intelligence Analysis
- **Analysis of competing hypotheses** — don't confirm, disconfirm
- **Structured analytic techniques** — reducing cognitive biases in intelligence work
- **The problem of deception** — how to detect when information is being manipulated

## Patterns in Military Intelligence That Apply Everywhere

1. **Intelligence isn't about data — it's about meaning.** More information doesn't help if you can't orient. The question is always "what does this mean?" not "what else can we get?"

2. **Surprise is a failure of orientation, not observation.** Most strategic surprises were not information failures — the data was there. They were interpretation failures.

3. **The best intelligence officers are comfortable with uncertainty.** They express confidence levels. They flag what they don't know. They resist the temptation to make the data fit a narrative.

4. **Red teaming works.** Assigning someone to argue the opposite case consistently produces better decisions. This applies to business strategy, product decisions, and personal life.

5. **Deception detection requires thinking about incentives.** What would the other side want you to believe? If you can answer that, you can check whether your current beliefs serve their interests.

## Connections to Other Domains

- **Business strategy** — competitive positioning, market intelligence, information asymmetry
- **Software** — threat modeling, security analysis, adversarial testing
- **Decision-making** — cognitive biases, confirmation bias, motivated reasoning
- **Personal relationships** — reading signals, understanding motivations, recognizing manipulation

## Dispatch Themes

When generating dispatches in this domain:
- Connect military concepts to business/personal situations
- Use historical examples to illuminate current decisions
- Draw parallels between intelligence analysis and everyday judgment
- Reference Musashi's void concept when discussing creative/strategic flexibility
- Apply Boyd's OODA loop to time-sensitive decisions
