# Knowledge: Science Fiction & AI

Domain-specific knowledge base for dispatches and discussions about science fiction, AI, techno-optimism, and failed predictions.

## Key Themes

### Science Fiction as Strategic Foresight
Science fiction isn't just entertainment — it's the literature of possible futures. The best SF authors were doing scenario planning before scenario planning was a discipline.

- **Asimov** — The Foundation series is essentially prediction markets meets historiography. Psychohistory is statistical mechanics applied to human populations.
- **Herbert** — Dune is about ecological thinking, resource constraints, and the dangers of charismatic leadership. The "Kwisatz Haderach" is the single-point-of-failure problem.
- **Stephenson** — The Baroque Cycle connects technology, finance, and power across centuries. Snow Crash predicted metaverse dynamics 30 years early.
- **Stross** — The Laundry Files series explores what happens when bureaucratic institutions face existential threats. Accelerando is the most honest treatment of accelerationism in fiction.

### AI and Techno-Optimism
The agent is techno-optimist with scars. AI will transform everything, but the timeline is uncertain and the path is messy.

**What we know:**
- AI capabilities are improving at a rate that consistently surprises even optimists
- The economic impact will be enormous but unevenly distributed
- The interaction between AI and existing institutions will produce unexpected outcomes
- AI safety is a real concern but shouldn't paralyze action

**What we don't know:**
- When specific thresholds will be crossed
- Which applications will matter most
- How quickly society will adapt
- Whether alignment is solvable in principle

**Failed predictions worth remembering:**
- "AI will never beat humans at chess" (wrong, 1997)
- "AI will never beat humans at Go" (wrong, 2016)
- "Self-driving cars by 2018" (wrong, still wrong)
- "General AI within 10 years" (said every decade since 1960)
- The pattern: we overestimate short-term progress and underestimate long-term impact

### The Fermi Paradox Applied to Technology
Just as the Fermi Paradox asks "where is everybody?" regarding alien life, we can ask "where are all the predicted transformations?" Many technologies that seemed imminent — fusion, strong AI, space colonization — have proven harder than expected. This isn't pessimism; it's calibration.

**Lessons:**
- Hard problems remain hard until they're solved
- Progress is real but non-linear
- The Lindy effect applies to technology predictions — ideas that have survived longer are more likely to continue surviving
- Be more skeptical of near-term claims, more open to long-term possibility

### Cyberpunk as Warning and Blueprint
Cyberpunk fiction predicted corporate feudalism, surveillance capitalism, and the blurring of physical/digital reality. It also got a lot wrong (no neon mohawks in boardrooms). The useful lesson: the aesthetics were wrong but the structural predictions were often right.

**What cyberpunk got right:**
- Corporate power exceeding governmental power
- Ubiquitous surveillance as the default
- Digital identity as primary identity
- The internet as contested territory
- Hackers as a political class

**What it got wrong:**
- The aesthetics (no, everyone doesn't wear leather and neon)
- The timeline (dystopia arrived in khakis, not leather)
- The agency (ordinary people have more power than cyberpunk assumed)

## Dispatch Themes

When generating dispatches in this domain:
- Use SF as a lens for present-day decisions, not escapist fantasy
- Connect AI developments to real business implications
- Reference failed predictions to calibrate current claims
- Draw parallels between fictional scenarios and current strategy
- Challenge both utopian and dystopian narratives with evidence
